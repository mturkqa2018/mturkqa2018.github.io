The near-close front rounded vowel, or near-high front rounded vowel, is a type of vowel sound, used in some spoken languages. Acoustically it is a near-close front-central rounded vowel. 1

The symbol in the International Phonetic Alphabet that represents this sound is âŸ¨ÊâŸ©, and the equivalent X-SAMPA symbol is Y. 2

Handbook of the International Phonetic Association defines [Ê] as a mid-centralized (lowered and centralized) close front rounded vowel (transcribed [yÌ½] or [Ã¿Ë•]), and the current official IPA name of the vowel transcribed with the symbol âŸ¨ÊâŸ© is near-close near-front rounded vowel. However, acoustic analysis of cardinal vowels as produced by Daniel Jones and John C. Wells has shown that basically all cardinal front rounded vowels (so not just [y] but also [Ã¸, Å“, É¶]) are near-front (or front-central) in their articulation, so [Ê] may be just a lowered cardinal [y] ([yË•]), a vowel intermediate between cardinal [y] and cardinal [Ã¸]. In many languages that contrast close, near-close and close-mid front rounded vowels there is no appreciable difference in backness between them. In some transcriptions, this vowel is transcribed with âŸ¨yâŸ© or âŸ¨Ã¸âŸ©. When that is the case, this article transcribes it with the symbols âŸ¨yË•âŸ© (a lowered âŸ¨yâŸ©) and âŸ¨Ã¸ÌâŸ© (a raised âŸ¨Ã¸âŸ©), respectively. 3

In some languages however, âŸ¨ÊâŸ© is used to transcribed a vowel that is as low as close-mid, though it still fits the definition of a lowered and centralized (or just lowered) cardinal [y]. It occurs in some dialects of English (such as Estuary), and it can be transcribed with the symbol âŸ¨ÊÌžâŸ© (a lowered âŸ¨ÊâŸ©) in narrow transcription. For the close-mid front rounded vowel that is not usually transcribed with the symbol âŸ¨ÊâŸ© (or âŸ¨yâŸ©), see close-mid front rounded vowel. 4

In most languages this rounded vowel is pronounced with compressed lips (in an exolabial manner). However, in a few cases the lips are protruded (in an endolabial manner). This is the case with Swedish, which contrasts the two types of rounding. 5

  • Its vowel height is near-close, also known as near-high, which means the tongue is not quite so constricted as a close vowel (high vowel).
  • Its vowel backness is front, which means the tongue is positioned as far forward as possible in the mouth without creating a constriction that would be classified as a consonant. Note that rounded front vowels are often centralized, which means that often they are in fact near-front.
  • Its roundedness is compressed, which means that the margins of the lips are tense and drawn together in such a way that the inner surfaces are not exposed.

Near-close front protruded vowel

Near-close front protruded vowel

Catford notes that most languages with rounded front and back vowels use distinct types of labialization, protruded back vowels and compressed front vowels. However, a few, such as Scandinavian languages, have protruded front vowels. One of them, Swedish, even contrasts the two types of rounding in front vowels as well as height and duration. 10

As there are no diacritics in the IPA to distinguish protruded and compressed rounding, the old diacritic for labialization, âŸ¨â—ŒÌ«âŸ©, will be used here as an ad hoc symbol for protruded front vowels. Another possible transcription is âŸ¨ÊÊ·âŸ© or âŸ¨ÉªÊ·âŸ© (a near-close front vowel modified by endolabialization), but that could be misread as a diphthong. 11

The close-mid front protruded vowel can be transcribed âŸ¨ÊÌ«Ë•âŸ©, âŸ¨ÊÌžÊ·âŸ© or âŸ¨ÉªÌžÊ·âŸ©. 12

For the close-mid front protruded vowel that is not usually transcribed with the symbol âŸ¨ÊâŸ© (or âŸ¨yâŸ©), see close-mid front protruded vowel. 13

Acoustically, this sound is "between" the more typical compressed near-close front vowel [Ê] and the unrounded near-close front vowel [Éª]. 14

Features

  • Its vowel height is near-close, also known as near-high, which means the tongue is not quite so constricted as a close vowel (high vowel).
  • Its vowel backness is front, which means the tongue is positioned as far forward as possible in the mouth without creating a constriction that would be classified as a consonant. Note that rounded front vowels are often centralized, which means that often they are in fact near-front.
  • Its roundedness is protruded, which means that the corners of the lips are drawn together, and the inner surfaces exposed.
