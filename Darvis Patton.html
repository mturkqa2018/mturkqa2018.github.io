Darvis "Doc" Darell Patton (born December 4, 1977) is a retired American track and field athlete who competed in sprinting events. He is a two-time US Champion in the 200-meter dash and won the silver medal in the event at the 2003 World Championships. He is a three-time Olympian and a four-time participant at the World Championships. 2

Patton has had much success with the American 4Ã—100-meter relay team, being a world gold medallist in 2003 and 2007, as well as a two-time Olympic silver medallist (2004 and 2012). He has also had misfortune in the relay, having been involved in the American team's disqualification at the 2008 Summer Olympics, the 2009 World Championships, and the 2011 World Championships. 3

A multi-talented athlete in the horizontal jumps and sprints, he initially focused on the 200Â m but, after missing 2005 and 2006 due to injury, he switched to the 100-meter dash. He won the 100Â m silver medal at the 2007 Pan American Games and reached the finals of the event at the 2008 Summer Olympics and the 2009 World Championships. He has broken the 10-second barrier and his personal record of 9.89Â seconds places him within the top-ten fastest Americans in the event. He also has bests of 20.03 for the 200Â m, 8.12Â m for the long jump, and 6.50 seconds for the 60-meter dash (a masters record for over-35 runners). 4

Career

Early career and college

Born in Dallas, Texas, the son of William Johnson and Dorrise Patton, Darvis Patton was a very versatile athlete from a young age and he competed in the long jump, triple jump and sprint events at Lake Highlands High School and TCU days. At the AAU Junior Olympic Games in 1998 he won the long jump, was runner-up in the triple jump and placed fourth in the 200-meter dash. He graduated from Garden City Community College that year and won an athletic scholarship at Texas Christian University. While there he began to focus more on the 100-meter dash and 200Â m under the coaching of Monte Stratton. He was a finalist at both those distances at the 2000 NCAA Men's Outdoor Track and Field Championship. He ended the year with personal records of 6.64 seconds for the 60-meter dash, 10.22 for the 100Â m, 20.29 for the 200Â m and 8.04Â mÂ (26Â ftÂ 4â€‹â„2Â in) for the long jump. He was also a semi-finalist in the 200Â m at the 2000 United States Olympic Trials. 5

In his second year of competition at Texas Christian he set lifetime indoor bests of 20.73 for the 200Â m and 7.85Â mÂ (25Â ftÂ 9Â in) for the long jump. He was a finalist in both events at the NCAA Men's Indoor Track and Field Championship. He improved outdoors too, setting personal records of 10.16 seconds for the 100Â m and 8.12Â mÂ (26Â ftÂ 7â€‹â„2Â in) for the long jump in Arlington, Texas. The 2001 NCAA Outdoor Championships saw him earn four All-American honours in one meet: he was sixth in the 100Â m, fourth in the long jump, third in the 200Â m and won the 4Ã—100-meter relay with the TCU Horned Frogs. He began to establish himself nationally at that year's USA Outdoor Track and Field Championships, where he came fourth in the 200Â m as well as seventh in the long jump. Patton was later upgraded to third in the 200Â m after Ramon Clay was banned for doping. 6

First World and Olympic medals

He had his first successes as a senior in 2002 when he was runner up in the 200Â m at both the Indoor and Outdoor US Championships. The retrospective disqualification of Ramon Clay meant he was the American outdoor 200Â m champion that year. There were no global track championships that year so he mainly competed on the international circuit. He won the 200Â m races in Glasgow and London and was runner-up at the Golden Gala and Memorial Van Damme meets. He improved his 200Â m best to 20.12 seconds in Brussels. 7

The following season saw him reach the global podium for the first time: he became the national champion in the 200Â m and went on to win the silver medal in that event at the 2003 World Championships in Athletics behind team mate John Capel. He also won a gold medal as part of the American men's 4Ã—100Â m relay team including Capel, Bernard Williams and J. J. Johnson. Moving entirely away from the long jump, Patton's sprinting times all improved that year: he had a personal record of 6.58 seconds in the 60Â m, ran ten seconds flat at the Weltklasse ZÃ¼rich meeting and ran 20.03 in the World Championships 200Â m semi-finals. On the circuit he was runner-up at the Athletissima and Meeting Gaz de France 200Â m races and placed fourth at the 2003 IAAF World Athletics Final. 8

After a fourth-place finish in the 200Â m at the 2004 US Olympic Trials, he was nominated as the substitute for the 4Ã—100Â m relay at the 2004 Summer Olympics. Patton helped the American team ease through the heats, recording a time of 38.02Â seconds. He was substituted for the newly crowned 100Â m Olympic champion Justin Gatlin for the final race, but the move backfired for the team as they finished a little slower (38.08) and had to settle for the silver medal behind Great Britain â€“ who they had easily beaten in the heats. Patton's 2005 and 2006 seasons were wrecked by significant groin and hip injuries and he only returned to major competitions in 2007. 9

Return from injury

1

Patton winning at the 2008 Penn Relays

Patton returned to action and focused on the shorter sprint distance that year. He finished eighth in the 100Â m final at the 2007 US Championships and withdrew from the 200Â m competition. Based on his performance, he was selected to represent the United States at the 2007 Pan American Games. He finished second in the 100Â m behind Churandy Martina and anchored the relay team to a bronze medal. He was chosen as part of the relay team for the 2007 World Championships and this time made the final after qualifying. The American team (with Wallace Spearmon, Tyson Gay, and Leroy Dixon) became the world champions in a world-leading time of 37.78Â seconds, fending off the Jamaican challengers who set a national record. 10

At the 2008 United States Olympic Trials, Patton achieved his lifetime best performance in the 100Â m, running 9.89 seconds in the quarter-finals. He went even quicker in the wind-assisted (+3.1Â m/s) final, clocking 9.84 seconds, and came in third place behind Tyson Gay and Walter Dix. He broke the 10-second barrier legally for the second time at the Herculis meet in Monaco, placing second behind Asafa Powell in 9.98 seconds. During the 2008 Summer Olympics in Beijing he was the slowest qualifier for the 100Â m final. In the final itself he ran 10.03 seconds and was again the slowest and thus the eighth placed athlete. Together with Rodney Martin, Travis Padgett and Tyson Gay he also competed at the 4 Ã— 100 m relay, but they did not finish due to a mistake in the baton exchange between Patton and Gay and they were eliminated. 11

The following year Patton came second to Michael Rodgers in a close 100Â m race at the 2009 US championships in June, running a windy 9.92 seconds. This won him a place on the American team for the 2009 World Championships in Athletics. He performed poorly in the final however, finishing last with a slow time of 10.34Â seconds due to an injury. The hopes of the American men's relay team were again dashed by an illegal baton exchange, this time between Patton and Shawn Crawford. Patton ended his season on a high: he ran a series of sub-10 runs including 9.95 for third at the Weltklasse, 9.94 to win the HanÅ¾ekoviÄ‡ Memorial, and a personal record-equalling run of 9.89Â seconds for third at the Shanghai Golden Grand Prix, behind Tyson Gay and Asafa Powell. He also placed third in the 100Â m at the Memorial Van Damme and the 2009 IAAF World Athletics Final. 12

Second Olympic medal

Patton's 2010 season was brought to an end in May due to another injury. He returned in 2011 and began racing more frequently in the 200Â m. At the 2011 USA Outdoor Track and Field Championships he was sixth in the 100Â m but managed second place to Walter Dix in the 200Â m. The 2011 World Championships in Athletics was one of his less successful global outings. He was eliminated in the 200Â m semi-finals and in the relay final he clashed with Britain's Harry Aikines-Aryeetey and failed to pass the baton successfully. He had season's bests of 9.94 seconds for the 100Â m and 20.25 seconds for the 200Â m. 13

The 2012 United States Olympic Trials he placed fifth in both the 100Â m and 200Â m. Although he had failed to gain an individual spot, he was chosen for the relay team. At the 2012 London Olympics he ran as a substitute runner in the qualifiers but even the weaker American team of Jeff Demps, Patton, Trell Kimmons, and Justin Gatlin broke the national record of 37.38 seconds. The American team for the final (bringing in Tyson Gay and Ryan Bailey) improved that record further to 37.04 seconds but was still second behind the world record-breaking Jamaican team. Patton received his second Olympic silver medal in the relay as a result. 14

He began 2013 with quick times, running 6.50 for the 60Â m (a world record for over-35 athletes) and a wind-aided 100Â m time of 9.75 seconds. 15

Personal life

He and his wife Crystal have a daughter, Dakota, and son, Darvis. Patton is a member of Big Brothers Big Sisters of America and serves as a mentor for youths. 16
