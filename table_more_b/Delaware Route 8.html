Delaware RouteÂ 8 (DEÂ 8) is a state highway located in Kent County in the U.S. state of Delaware. It runs from Maryland RouteÂ 454 (MDÂ 454) at the Maryland border in Marydel east to an intersection with DEÂ 9 north of Little Creek. The route passes through rural areas of western Kent County before heading through Delaware's capital city, Dover, on Forrest Avenue and Division Street. East of Dover, the road passes through more rural areas. DEÂ 8 intersects DEÂ 44 in Pearsons Corner; DEÂ 15, U.S. RouteÂ 13 Alternate (USÂ 13 Alt.), and USÂ 13 in Dover; and DEÂ 1 at a partial interchange east of Dover. The road was built as a state highway west of Dover by 1924 and east of Dover by 1931. The DEÂ 8 designation was given to the road by 1936. 2

Route description

DEÂ 8 begins at the Maryland border in Marydel, where the road continues into the town of Marydel, Maryland as MDÂ 454. From the state line, the route heads southeast on two-lane undivided Halltown Road, passing a few homes and businesses. The road leaves Marydel and curves northeast through a mix of farmland and woodland with some homes. In Pearsons Corner, DEÂ 8 intersects the eastern terminus of DEÂ 44, where it turns to the east past businesses and the name changes to Forrest Avenue upon crossing Pearsons Corner Road. From here, the route runs through more rural areas with some development. This area of Kent County is home to many Amish farms, homes, and businesses. 3

Eastbound DEÂ 8 (Forrest Avenue) in the western part of Dover

DEÂ 8 crosses into the city of Dover, where it passes to the north of Dover High School before it widens from a two-lane road to a five-lane road with a center left-turn lane. The road runs past homes and businesses in the western part of Dover, intersecting Kenton Road and DEÂ 15. The name changes to Forest Street upon crossing DEÂ 15 and the route continues east onto West Division Street at the point Forest Street splits to the southeast to lead to Loockerman Street and downtown Dover. At this point, DEÂ 8 narrows to a two-lane road. The route crosses the Delmarva Central Railroad's Delmarva Subdivision railroad line and continues past a mix of homes and businesses to the north of downtown area. The road crosses USÂ 13 Alt. and passes to the south of Wesley College before intersecting State Street, where the name becomes East Division Street. The route intersects Kings Highway SW north of the Delaware Governor's Mansion and the name changes to Kings Highway NE. DEÂ 8 crosses the St. Jones River and Kings Highway NE splits to the north, with DEÂ 8 once again becoming East Division Street. The road heads between abandoned industrial areas to the north and residential neighborhoods to the south, widening to four lanes. 4

DEÂ 8 intersects USÂ 13 in a commercial area and continues through the residential eastern part of Dover as a two-lane road. The name changes to North Little Creek Road and the route heads through less dense areas of homes with some farmland. At the eastern edge of the city, the roadway features a partial interchange with the DEÂ 1 toll road, providing access to and from the north. After this interchange, the road leaves Dover and continues east through open agricultural areas, crossing the Little River. DEÂ 8 reaches its eastern terminus at an intersection with DEÂ 9 just north of the town of Little Creek. 5

The section of the route between DEÂ 1 east of Dover and DEÂ 9 north of Little Creek is designated as a spur of the Delaware's Bayshore Byway, a Delaware Byway. DEÂ 8 has an annual average daily traffic count ranging from a high of 21,663Â vehicles at the DEÂ 15 intersection to a low of 858Â vehicles at the Little Creek border near the eastern terminus. The portion of DEÂ 8 between DEÂ 44 and DEÂ 15 is part of the National Highway System. 6

History

2

DEÂ 8 eastbound past the eastern terminus of DEÂ 44 in Pearsons Corner

By 1920, what is now DEÂ 8 existed as an unimproved county road. The route was completed as a state highway between the Maryland border in Marydel and Dover by 1924. By 1925, the road was proposed as a state highway between Dover and Little Creek. This state highway was completed by 1931. DEÂ 8 was assigned to its current alignment between the Maryland border in Marydel and DEÂ 9 north of Little Creek by 1936. 7

On September 5, 2002, a partial interchange opened at the DEÂ 1 toll road in Dover, utilizing existing emergency vehicle ramps. This interchange was included in the initial plans for the highway but was dropped due to low traffic volumes. As part of building the interchange, DelDOT purchased development rights to adjacent land parcels in order to prevent additional development in the area of the interchange. In 2012, the Interdenominational Ministerial Alliance pushed for the city of Dover to rename the Division Street portion of DEÂ 8 to Martin Luther King Jr. Boulevard after civil rights leader Martin Luther King Jr. However, local merchants opposed the renaming. The Dover city council instead voted to rename Court Street, Duke of York Street, and William Penn Street near Delaware Legislative Hall to Martin Luther King Jr. Boulevard. 8
