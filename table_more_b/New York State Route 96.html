New York State RouteÂ 96 (NYÂ 96) is a 126.01-mile (202.79Â km) northwestâ€“southeast state highway in the Finger Lakes region of New York in the United States. The southern terminus of the route is at an interchange with NYÂ 17 (Future I-86) in the Southern Tier village of Owego, Tioga County. Its northern terminus is at a junction with East Main Street in the city of Rochester, Monroe County. Between the two endpoints, NYÂ 96 passes through the city of Ithaca and the villages of Waterloo, Victor, and Pittsford. NYÂ 96 is signed northâ€“south for its entire length, although most of the route in Ontario County travels in an eastâ€“west direction. 2

All of NYÂ 96, except from Candor to Ithaca and from northwest of Victor to Pittsford, was originally designated as part of New York State RouteÂ 15 in 1924. NYÂ 15 was originally routed on modern NYÂ 96B between Candor and Ithaca, and modern NYÂ 64 and NYÂ 251 between Victor and Pittsford. NYÂ 15 was renumbered to New York State RouteÂ 2 c.â€‰1939 to eliminate duplication with U.S. RouteÂ 15 (USÂ 15). NYÂ 2 was subsequently redesignated as NYÂ 96 in 1942 as the alignments of NYÂ 2 and NYÂ 96, a route in Rensselaer County, were swapped. NYÂ 96 was realigned again in the early 1950s, this time between Candor and Ithaca, to serve the village of Spencer west of Candor. Near Rochester, NYÂ 96 followed what is now InterstateÂ 490 (I-490) for a short time during the 1950s and early 1960s. 3

Route description

Most of NYÂ 96's 126 miles (203Â km) are maintained by the New York State Department of Transportation (NYSDOT); however, three sections of the route in Tompkins County and Monroe County are maintained by local highway departments. In the Tompkins County city of Ithaca, NYÂ 96 is city-maintained from the southern city line to the start of the Fulton/Meadow Street one-way couplet, at which point maintenance of the highway reverts to NYSDOT. The route is also maintained by Ithaca from Park Road to the western city limits. The last locally owned section is in the Monroe County city of Rochester, where the entirety of NYÂ 96 within the city is city-maintained. 4

Tioga County

Though unsigned at this point, NYÂ 96 begins at the eastbound onramp to NYÂ 17 at exitÂ 64 on Southside Drive across the Susquehanna River from the Tioga County village of Owego. The route remains unsigned until the intersection of Court Street and Southside Drive, the western terminus of NYÂ 434. NYÂ 434 continues east along Southside Drive while NYÂ 96, now fully signed, turns north onto Court Street and crosses the Susquehanna River. On the opposite side of the river, NYÂ 96 intersects Front Street. The configuration of NYÂ 96 between Front Street and East Main Street is unorthodox in that NYÂ 96 splits at Front Street to follow a one-way couplet around the Tioga County Courthouse to Main Street, where both streets terminate. NYÂ 96 turns onto Main Street for half a block to North Avenue, where it resumes its northward path. The route shares the parallel one-way streets with NYÂ 17C, which enters Owego from the west via Main Street and leaves via Front Street to the east. 5

Tompkins County

The two routes remain concurrent as they snake to the northwest through Tompkins County. Southwest of the city of Ithaca in the town of Ithaca, NYÂ 34 and NYÂ 96 meet NYÂ 13. The three routes continue northeast through the town, intersecting NYÂ 327 and NYÂ 13A before crossing over the inlet of Cayuga Lake and entering the city of Ithaca on Meadow Street. Here, NYÂ 96B reconnects to its parent at the junction of Clinton and Meadow Streets. North of this point, Meadow Street splits into a one-way couplet, with Fulton Street carrying southbound traffic and Meadow Street handling northbound traffic. NYÂ 79, also routed on a one-way couplet here, crosses NYÂ 96 at Green and Seneca Streets, with NYÂ 79 eastbound using one block of Fulton Street to travel from State Street to Green Street. 7

Seneca County

NYÂ 96 proceeds northwest through a lightly populated section of Seneca County and the town of Covert to the village of Interlaken, home to the southern terminus of NYÂ 96A, a more westerly alternate route of NYÂ 96 between Interlaken and Geneva. Outside of the village, NYÂ 96 continues on a northwesterly path for another 4.5 miles (7.2Â km) to the Ovidâ€“Romulus town line, where it turns due west for 2 miles (3.2Â km) to access the village of Ovid. The route mostly bypasses the village, with NYÂ 96A and NYÂ 414 serving as its Main Street instead. The three routes meet at a junction on the northern fringe of the community, at which point NYÂ 96A leaves NYÂ 414 and turns west to follow the town line toward Seneca Lake while NYÂ 96 joins NYÂ 414 and heads north into the town of Romulus. 10

Midway between the village of Ovid and the hamlet of Romulus, the two routes split, allowing NYÂ 414 to continue due north to Seneca Falls. While on Washington Street, the route passes over the Cayugaâ€“Seneca Canal and enters the village's center, changing names to Virginia Street in the process. Not far to the north, NYÂ 96 heads into Waterloo's central business district, built up around the junction of Virginia and Main (USÂ 20 and NYÂ 5) Streets. Past RoutesÂ 5 andÂ 20, NYÂ 96 continues north as a residential street to the village line, where the route turns to the west with a slight trend to the north. From this point northwestward to Rochester, NYÂ 96 passes through substantially more developed areas. The section of NYÂ 96 between exitÂ 29 and NYÂ 250 in Perinton serves as a major commercial strip, anchored by the presence of Eastview Mall, one of the largest malls in the Rochester area and the largest east of the city. Also present along this stretch of NYÂ 96 are a series of plazas, beginning with one just north of I-490 featuring a Walmart and a Kohl's. Continuing northward, NYÂ 96 runs along the eastern edge of Eastview Mall and passes two more plazas, one of which is developed around a Kmart. The highway gradually turns northwest toward the county line as it passes the mall, and crosses into Monroe County at an intersection leading to Eastview Commons, a plaza separated from the mall by high-voltage power lines and a large ditch. 16

West of Winton, NYÂ 96 passes through the East Avenue Historic District, a primarily residential area with historic upper-class houses, including the George Eastman House. History

8

In 1908, the New York State Legislature created RouteÂ 36, an unsigned legislative route that extended from Owego to Seneca Falls via Candor, Ithaca, and Ovid. South of Romulus, RouteÂ 36 utilized what is now NYÂ 96 and NYÂ 96B. When the first set of posted routes in New York were assigned in 1924, the Owegoâ€“Interlaken and Ovidâ€“Romulus portions of legislative RouteÂ 36 became part of NYÂ 15, which began in Owego and proceeded northwest from Romulus to Rochester by way of Waterloo, Phelps, Victor, Mendon, and Pittsford. From Mendon to Rochester, NYÂ 15 followed the path of legislative RouteÂ 14, another highway dating back to 1908 that continued south from Mendon on what is now NYÂ 64 and used Monroe Avenue between Pittsford and Rochester. Another section of NYÂ 15â€”from the village of Phelps east to NYÂ 14â€”utilized what had been designated as part of legislative RouteÂ 6-a from 1911 to 1921. 22

By 1926, NYÂ 31 was assigned across western and central New York, utilizing Monroe Avenue from downtown Rochester to Pittsford. Although NYÂ 96's modern routing via East Avenue was state-maintained and formerly part of legislative RouteÂ 20 from current NYÂ 31F westward, NYÂ 15 initially remained on Monroe Avenue, creating an overlap between NYÂ 15 and NYÂ 31. It was realigned at some point between 1927 and 1932 to follow East Avenue to Rochester instead. In southern Seneca County, NYÂ 15 initially passed through Lodi on its way from Interlaken to Ovid. It was realigned in the late 1920s to bypass Lodi to the northeast on the former alignment of legislative RouteÂ 36. 23

In the 1930 renumbering of state highways in New York, NYÂ 15 basically remained intact. The only change made at this time was the straightening out of the Victorâ€“Pittsford segment, which now bypassed Mendon in favor of a more direct alignment between the two villages via Bushnell's Basin. The former alignment of NYÂ 15 became part of NYÂ 251 east of Mendon and part of NYÂ 64 north of the hamlet. USÂ 15 was extended into New York c.â€‰1939; it replaced NYÂ 2, which had extended from the Pennsylvania state line to Rochester. NYÂ 15 was renumbered to NYÂ 2 to eliminate numerical duplication with the U.S. Highway. 24

The NYÂ 96 designation was originally assigned to present-day NYÂ 2 from NYÂ 7 in Troy to RouteÂ 2 at the Massachusetts state line. In 1942, the alignments of NYÂ 2 and NYÂ 96 were swapped, placing NYÂ 96 on the Owegoâ€“Rochester routing. The only major change to NYÂ 96 since that time came in the early 1950s, when the route was realigned between Candor and Ithaca to follow a new routing via Spencer. The Candorâ€“Spencer portion of the alignment had been part of NYÂ 53 during the 1920s and part of NYÂ 223 since 1930. NYÂ 223 was truncated to its current eastern terminus at NYÂ 224 near Van Etten as part of NYÂ 96's realignment. Between Spencer and Ithaca, NYÂ 96 overlapped with NYÂ 34, which had occupied that segment of highway since the 1930 renumbering. 25

In the 1950s, NYÂ 96 was temporarily moved onto the Eastern Expressway as sections of the freeway opened to traffic. The first section extended from Bushnell's Basin to NYÂ 31F and opened to traffic in tNovemberÂ 1955, at which time NYÂ 96 was routed onto the new highway and NYÂ 252 and NYÂ 64 were extended eastward and northward, respectively, to cover NYÂ 96's old surface alignment. NYÂ 31F, meanwhile, was truncated to begin at the expressway. A northwest extension to what is now the Can of Worms was completed c.â€‰1957 as a realignment of NYÂ 96, resulting in the re-extension of NYÂ 31F to its original terminus and an extension of NYÂ 64 along East Avenue to the eastern edge of Rochester. NYÂ 64, NYÂ 96, and NYÂ 252 were restored to their pre-1950s alignments c.â€‰1961 when the freeway was designated as I-490. 26
