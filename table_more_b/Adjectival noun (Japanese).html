In descriptions of the Japanese language, an adjectival noun, adjectival, or na-adjective is a noun that can function as an adjective by taking the particle ã€œãª -na. Adjectival nouns constitute one of several Japanese word classes that can be considered equivalent to adjectives. 1

Terminology

The terminology used to refer to this word-class is inconsistent. The Japanese name is å½¢å®¹å‹•è©ž keiyÅ-dÅshi, which literally means "adjectival verb". This is not necessarily at odds with the English term adjectival noun, since in traditional Japanese grammar, keiyÅ-dÅshi includes the copula, while the adjectival noun in the analysis described here does not include the copula. For example, in the traditional grammar, kirei da is a keiyÅ-dÅshi and kirei is its stem; in the analysis here, kirei is an adjectival noun and kirei da is its combination with the copula. Considering the copula is a kind of verb and kirei is a kind of noun syntactically, both terminologies make sense. 3

Grammatically, these words are nouns, or more technically, nominals, which function attributively (like adjectives)Â â€“ the main differences being that nouns take a ã€œã® -no suffix when acting attributively, while these words take a ã€œãª -na suffix when acting attributively, and that most of these words cannot be used as the agent or patient (i.e. subject) of a sentence, but otherwise behaving essentially identically grammatically. Thus, they are variously referred to as "adjectival verbs" (literal translation), "adjectival nouns" (nouns that function adjectivally), na-adjectives (function as adjectives, take na), and na-nominals (nominals that take na). For example, Eleanor Harz Jorden refers to them as na-nominals in her textbook Japanese: The Spoken Language. 4

In fact, by some analyses, nouns and na-nominals are fundamentally grammatically the same, where ã€œã® vs. ã€œãª when used attributively is simply a conventional stylistic complementary distribution, with ã€œã®ï¼ã€œãª being allomorphs. This view is reinforced by the fact that some words, such as ç‰¹åˆ¥ tokubetsu "special", can take either a ã€œã® or a ã€œãª, depending on the phrase. Ultimately, ã€œãª is an abbreviation of ã€œã«ã‚ã‚‹, used to use a noun attributively (compare modern ã€œã§ã‚ã‚‹, which is a more recent form), while ã€œã® is the genitive case; see etymology, below. 5

Characterization

In traditional Japanese grammar, adjectival nouns are considered "inflectional", katsuyÅ, like verbs and i-adjectives, rather than non-inflectional hikatsuyÅgo (éžæ´»ç”¨èªž) or mukatsuyÅgo (ç„¡æ´»ç”¨èªž), like nouns. 6

This is a point of disagreement in current Japanese grammar, and authors such as Uehara (1998) argue that instead, adjectival nouns should be classed with nouns as non-inflectional. 7

The claim that na-adjectives are inflectional rests on the claim that the syllable da 'is', usually regarded as a "copula verb", is really a suffixâ€”an inflection. Thus, hon 'book', generates a one-word sentence, honda 'it is a book', not a two-word sentence, hon da. However, numerous constructions seem to be incompatible with the suffixal copula claim. 8

(1) Reduplication for emphasis
Hora! Hon, hon! 'See, it is a book!'
Hora! Kirei, kirei! 'See, it is pretty!'
Hora! Furui, furui! 'See, it is old!' (the adjectival inflection -i cannot be left off)
Hora! Iku, iku! 'See, it does go!' (the verbal inflection -u cannot be left off)
(2) Questions. In Japanese, questions are formed by adding the particle ka (or in colloquial speech, just by changing the intonation of the sentence).
Hon/kirei ka? 'Is it a book?Â ; Is it pretty?'
Furu-i/Ik-u ka? 'Is it old?Â ; Does it go?' (the inflections cannot be left off)
(3) Several auxiliary verbs, e.g., mitai, 'looks like it's'
Hon mitai da; Kirei mitai da 'It seems to be a book; It seems to be pretty'
Furu-i mitai da; Ik-u mitai da 'It seems to be old; It seems to go'

On the basis of such constructions, Uehara (1998) finds that the copula is indeed an independent word, and that regarding the parameters on which i-adjectives share the syntactic pattern of verbs, the nominal adjectives pattern with pure nouns instead. 9

taru adjectives

Main article: taru adjective

In Late Old Japanese (below), a separate kind of tari adjectival nouns developed alongside the existing nari ones (nari, tari were the conclusive forms, while naru, taru were the attributive forms). Etymology

Japanese adjectival nouns differ in etymological origin from adjectival verbs. Whereas adjectival verbs are almost entirely native in origin, the class of adjectival nouns comprises mostly foreign loanwords and a small subset of polymorphemic native words. All words listed in this section take the attributive -na and predicative -da copula. 21

Copula -na/-da

All forms of the copula (the vehicle for the inflection of adjectival nouns) can be considered to derive from two infinitive forms, ni and to. Because the copula lacked any other forms, secondary conjugations with the verb ari were used. The original ni ari and to ari contracted to form nari and tari. To derive the modern forms na and da, changes such as the following have been proposed. 28

For attributive na (rentaikei): 29

  • ni aru > naru > na

For predicative da (shÅ«shikei): 30

  • ni te ari > de ari > de a > da

In some regions, these changes progressed differently, resulting in forms such as ja (ChÅ«goku, Shikoku, or KyÅ«shÅ«; particularly common in Hiroshima) or ya (associated with Kansai dialect, particularly ÅŒsaka.) 31

The infinitive form ni is still in widespread use (e.g. hen ni naru, "become strange"), but the form to has become a much rarer alternative. 32

Internal Properties

The internal properties of Japanese adjectival nouns can be analysed either through a lexical features approach or through a Distributed Morphology approach. 33
