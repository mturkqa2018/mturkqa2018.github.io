Route description

Syracuse

NYÂ 92 begins as a city-maintained highway at an intersection with USÂ 11 (State Street) in downtown Syracuse. The route heads eastward on East Genesee Street, passing under I-81 before splitting into a one-way couplet at Almond Street. For the next two blocks, the eastbound and westbound lanes of NYÂ 92 are separated by a small park situated within the block formed by Almond Street, Forman Avenue, and both directions of Genesee Street. The couplet ends just east of Forman Avenue, at which point NYÂ 92 continues on as a highway two lanes wide with dedicated cycle lanes in each direction. 4

1

Heading east past Forman Park

At Lexington Avenue, NYÂ 92 begins to turn to the southeast as it heads through more residential areas of the city. At East Avenue, a local street just west of Nottingham Senior High School, a center lane begins, enabling left turns from both directions. The route passes north of the school and St. Mary's Cemetery and south of LeMoyne College before exiting the city limits. At this point, maintenance of the route shifts from Syracuse to the New York State Department of Transportation (NYSDOT). Now in DeWitt, NYÂ 92 continues past several blocks lined with homes to an intersection with Erie Boulevard, a six-lane divided highway that carries NYÂ 5 through eastern Syracuse. The boulevard ends here, however, and NYÂ 5 turns to join NYÂ 92 eastward along Genesee Street. 5

East of Syracuse

Not far to the east of this junction, NYÂ 5 and NYÂ 92 meet I-481 by way of a cloverleaf interchange. East of the interchange, Genesee Street widens to six lanes (three in each direction) as it heads along a commercial strip anchored by a large plaza featuring a Wegmans Food Markets store. NYÂ 5 and NYÂ 92 split at the east end of the strip in the hamlet of Lyndon. While NYÂ 5 continues east along Genesee Street to Fayetteville, NYÂ 92 heads southeast along the two-lane Highbridge Road and bypasses Fayetteville to the southwest. 6

The route continues into the town of Manlius, where it crosses over Limestone Creek at the hamlet of High Bridge. Past this point, the amount of development along the highway increases as it approaches an intersection with NYÂ 257 just inside of the Manlius village limits. Here, NYÂ 92 becomes four lanes wide once again as it heads along the mostly commercial Fayette Street toward the village center. In the heart of Manlius, NYÂ 92 overlaps with NYÂ 173 for about 750 feet (230Â m) past another commercial strip along Seneca Street before resuming its southeasterly progression at Washington Street. It becomes Cazenovia Road upon exiting the village. 7

3

Buildings along NYÂ 92 and NYÂ 173 in Manlius

Outside of the village of Manlius, the amount of development along the route decreases as the homes lining the route become more spaced out. NYÂ 92 follows Limestone Creek southeastward through the towns of Manlius and Pompey to the Madison County line, where the creek turns south to run through Pompey Hollow, an area of flatlands situated on the Onondaga County side of the line. The highway continues on into Madison County, where it becomes known as Syracuse Road as it heads through the town of Cazenovia. Most of NYÂ 92 in Madison County passes through rural, undeveloped areas; however, the section just north of its intersection with USÂ 20 runs along Cazenovia Lake and serves several lakeside homes. NYÂ 92 ends upon intersecting USÂ 20 at the southwestern tip of the lake just west of the village of Cazenovia. 8

At Columbus Avenue, NYÂ 92 turned north and followed that street to Erie Boulevard, where it ended at NYÂ 5. NYÂ 92 was extended west along Genesee Street to its current terminus at USÂ 11 in April 1935. 9

The segment of NYÂ 92 between Manlius and Cazenovia gained another designation in 1937 when it became part of NYÂ 20N, a northerly alternate route of USÂ 20 that was concurrent with other state routes for its entire length. In the early 1950s, the portions of NYÂ 92 west of DeWitt and from Manlius to Cazenovia also became part of NYÂ 20SY, another alternate route of USÂ 20 that veered even farther north than NYÂ 20N in order to serve downtown Syracuse. NYÂ 20SY was realigned slightly between 1952 and 1954 to follow NYÂ 5 through eastern Syracuse instead. 10

While most of NYÂ 20SY overlapped other state routes, there were two sections where NYÂ 20SY was the sole designation assigned to the highway it ran along. One of the two segments was near Fayetteville, where NYÂ 20SY left NYÂ 5 and NYÂ 92 west of the village and bypassed Fayetteville to the southwest on Highbridge Road before rejoining NYÂ 92 just north of Manlius. The NYÂ 20N and NYÂ 20SY designations were removed in 1961, at which time NYÂ 92 was realigned to bypass Fayetteville by way of NYÂ 20SY's former routing southwest of the village. 11
