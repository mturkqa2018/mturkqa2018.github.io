RzeszÃ³w (Polish: [ËˆÊÉ›Ê‚uf] (listen); Latin: Resovia; German: Resche) is the largest city in southeastern Poland and eighteenth in the whole country, with a population of 190,203 (30 June 2018). It is located on both sides of the WisÅ‚ok River in the heartland of the Sandomierz Basin. RzeszÃ³w has been the capital of the Subcarpathian Voivodeship since 1 January 1999, and is also the seat of RzeszÃ³w County. 1

The history of RzeszÃ³w begins in 1354, when it received city rights and privileges by Casimir III the Great. Local trade routes connecting the European Continent with the Middle East and the Ottoman Empire resulted in the city's early prosperity and development. In the 16th century, RzeszÃ³w had a connection with GdaÅ„sk and the Baltic Sea. It also experienced growth in commerce and craftsmanship, especially under local rulers and noblemen. During World War II RzeszÃ³w's large Jewish community perished in the Holocaust. 2

RzeszÃ³w has found its place in the group of the most elite cities in Poland, with growing number of investments, rapid progress and a very high standard of living. In 2011 Forbes awarded RzeszÃ³w with the second place in the ranking of the most attractive semi-large cities for business. Moreover, the city is home to a number of higher education schools and foreign consulates. RzeszÃ³w is also developing as a regional tourist destination; its Old Town, Main Market Square, churches and synagogues are among the best preserved in the country. 3

In recent years, the population of RzeszÃ³w has grown from 159,000 (2005) to over 190,000 (2018). Further plans for extending the city's borders include incorporating surrounding counties to strengthen its function as a metropolitan centre in southeastern Poland. 4

RzeszÃ³w is served by an international airport and is a member of Eurocities. 5

History

In the area of RzeszÃ³w, the first humans appeared in the late Paleolithic Age; archeologists have excavated a tool made in that period at site RzeszÃ³w 25. In the mid-6th century BC, the first farmers came to the area of the city, most likely through the Moravian Gate. Later on, RzeszÃ³w was a settlement of the Lusatian culture, which was followed by the Przeworsk culture. 6

In the 5th century, the first Slavs appeared in the area, which is confirmed by numerous archeological findings. Most probably, RzeszÃ³w was then inhabited by the Vistulans. Some time between 11th and 13th century the town was conquered and subsequently annexed by the East Slavic Ruthenians. Polish princes of the Piast dynasty annexed it in 1264 and in TarnÃ³w, there was a meeting of Prince BolesÅ‚aw V the Chaste, and Prince Daniel of Kingdom of Galiciaâ€“Volhynia, during which both sides agreed that the border would go between RzeszÃ³w and Czudec (RzeszÃ³w belonged to Kingdom of Galiciaâ€“Volhynia, while Czudec and StrzyÅ¼Ã³w to Lesser Poland). 7

After unification of Poland (see Testament of BolesÅ‚aw III Wrymouth), RzeszÃ³w remained in Ruthenian hands until 1340, when Casimir III the Great annexed Red Ruthenia, inviting his knights to govern the newly acquired land. According to some sources, at that time RzeszÃ³w was inhabited by the Walddeutsche, and was called Rishof (during World War II, the Germans renamed it Reichshof). The town was granted Magdeburg rights, it had a parish church, a market place and a cemetery, and its total area was some 1,5Â km. Magdeburg rights granted RzeszÃ³w's local authorities the permission to punish criminals, build fortifications and tax merchants. 8

In 1458 RzeszÃ³w was burned by the Vlachs and the Turkic Tatars. In 1502 the Tatars destroyed it again. Earlier, in 1427, RzeszÃ³w had burned to the ground in a big fire, but the town recovered after these events, thanks to its favorable location on the main West â€“ East (KrakÃ³w â€“ LwÃ³w) and North â€“ South (Lublin â€“ Slovakia) trade routes. In the 15th century the first Jews settled in RzeszÃ³w. The 16th century was the time of prosperity for the town, especially when RzeszÃ³w belonged to MikoÅ‚aj Spytek LigÄ™za (since the 1580s), who invested in infrastructure, building a castle, a Bernardine church and a monastery. RzeszÃ³w then had some 2,500 inhabitants, with a rapidly growing Jewish community. The town was granted several royal rights, including the privilege to organise several markets a year. At that time, RzeszÃ³w finally grew beyond its medieval borders, marked by fortifications. 9

In 1638 RzeszÃ³w passed into the hands of the powerful and wealthy Lubomirski family, becoming the centre of its vast properties. At first, the town prospered and in 1658, first college was opened there, which now operates as High School Nr 1. The period of prosperity ended, and furthermore, there were several fires and wars, which destroyed the town. RzeszÃ³w was first captured by the Swedes during The Deluge, then by the troops of George II RÃ¡kÃ³czi leading to the Treaty of Radnot. During the Great Northern War, the Swedes again captured RzeszÃ³w, in 1702, then several different armies occupied the town, ransacking it and destroying houses. 10

In the mid-eighteenth century, the town's population was composed of Poles (Roman Catholics) and Yiddish Jews in almost equal numbers (50.1% and 49.8%, respectively). 11

RzeszÃ³w under Austrian rule

In 1772, following first partition of Poland, RzeszÃ³w became part of the Austrian Empire, to which it belonged for 146 years. In the late 18th century, RzeszÃ³w had 3,000 inhabitants. By the mid-19th century, the population grew to around 7,500, with 40% of them Jewish. In 1858, Galician Railway of Archduke Charles Louis reached RzeszÃ³w, which resulted in further development of the town. In 1888 first telephone lines were opened, in 1900 â€“ gas street lamps, and in 1911 â€“ power plant and water system. The population grew to 23,000, with half of inhabitants being Jews. A number of modern building were constructed, most of them in Secession style. 12

During World War I, several battles took place in the area of the town. RzeszÃ³w was home to a large garrison of the Austro-Hungarian Army, and in the city of PrzemyÅ›l, located nearby, there was a major fortress. During the Battle of Galicia in the late summer of 1914, Russian troops moved towards RzeszÃ³w, and on 21 September, they captured it. First Russian occupation lasted only 16 days, ending after an attack of the Austrians, on 4 October. Under Russian pressure, the Austrians were unable to keep the town, and on 7 November, the Russians again appeared in RzeszÃ³w. In late fall of 1914, the frontline was established between TarnÃ³w and Gorlice, and RzeszÃ³w became an important center of the Imperial Russian Army, with large magazines of food and ammunition located there. Russian occupation lasted until May 1915. 13

After the Russians were pushed out of Galicia, RzeszÃ³w remained outside of the area of military activities. Austrian administration returned, but wartime reality and destruction of the town had a negative effect on the population, and the quality of life deteriorated. 14

Interwar period and Second World War

On 12 October 1918, RzeszÃ³w's mayor, together with the town council, sent a message to Warsaw, announcing loyalty to the independent Polish government. On November 1st, after clashes with German and Austrian troops, RzeszÃ³w was liberated, and the next day, mayor Roman Krogulski took a pledge of allegiance to the Polish state. During World War I, some 200 residents of RzeszÃ³w died, rail infrastructure was destroyed, as well as approximately 60 houses. 15

In 1920, RzeszÃ³w became capital of a county in the LwÃ³w Voivodeship. The town grew, and creation of the Central Industrial Region had an enormous impact on RzeszÃ³w. It became a major center of defense industry, with PZL RzeszÃ³w opening there in 1937, it also was a home to a large garrison of the Polish Army, with the 10th Motorized Cavalry Brigade stationed there. In 1939, RzeszÃ³w had 40,000 inhabitants, but its dynamic growth was stopped by the Invasion of Poland. 16

On 6 September 1939, RzeszÃ³w was bombed by the Luftwaffe. The town was defended by the 10 Cavalry Brigade and 24th Uhlan Regiment from KraÅ›nik. German attack began on 6 September in the afternoon, and the Wehrmacht entered the city on the next day in the morning. RzeszÃ³w, renamed into Reichshof, became part of the General Government, in 1941 a ghetto was opened there, whose Jewish inhabitants were later murdered in BeÅ‚Å¼ec extermination camp. 17

During the war, RzeszÃ³w was a main center of Polish resistance (Home Army, AK), with RzeszÃ³w Inspectorate of the AK covering several counties. On 25 May, during Action Kosba, Home Army soldiers killed on RzeszÃ³w street Gestapo henchmen, Friederich Pottenbaum and Hans Flaschke. In the summer 1944, during Operation Tempest, units of the Home Army attacked German positions in the town, and on 2 August, RzeszÃ³w was in the hands of the Home Army. Polish authorities loyal to the London Government tried to negotiate with the Soviets, but without success. NKVD immediately opened a prison in the cellars of the RzeszÃ³w Castle, sending there a number of Home Army soldiers. In the night of 7/9 October 1944, Home Army unit under Åukasz CiepliÅ„ski attacked the castle, trying to release 400 inmates kept there. The attack failed, and CiepliÅ„ski was captured and subsequently executed in 1951. 18

The Holocaust

6

Until the outbreak of World War II the Jews of RzeszÃ³w numbered 14,000, more than one-third of the total ethnic Polish population. The town was occupied by the German Army on 10 September 1939 and was renamed as "Reichshof". German persecution of the Jews began almost immediately; by the end of 1939, there were 10 forced labour camps in the RzeszÃ³w region and many Jews became slave labourers. Jews were forced to live in the Gestapo-controlled ghetto. Many Jews managed to flee to Soviet-occupied Poland. By June 1940, the number of Jews in RzeszÃ³w had decreased to 11,800, of whom 7,800 were pre-war residents of the city; the rest were from surrounding villages. Life in the ghetto was impossible and hundreds died. During the war some 20,000 Jews were murdered in the ghetto in RzeszÃ³w. This number includes thousands who were sent to RzeszÃ³w only to be deported or murdered soon after arrival. 19

In the fall of 1943 most Jewish slave labor was transported in Holocaust trains to the newly reopened Szebnie concentration camp. A month later, on 5 November 1943, some 2,800 Jews were sent from there, to meet their demise in Auschwitz. Of RzeszÃ³w's 14,000 Jews, only 100 survived the war in RzeszÃ³w itself, hiding all over Poland, and in various camps. After the war an additional 600 RzeszÃ³w Jews returned from the Soviet Union. Almost all of them subsequently left the city and the country. 20
